However, these freedoms are subject to public order, health, and morality. The Place of Worship act 1991

The Place of Worship (Special Provisions) Act, 1991 is an Indian law that prohibits the conversion of any place of worship and provides for the maintenance of the religious character of any place of worship as it existed on the 15th day of August, 1947. Here are some key points about the Place of Worship Act, 1991:

The Act was introduced to prevent the conversion of religious places of worship and to maintain their religious character as it existed on August 15, 1947.

The Act applies to all places of worship, including mosques, temples, churches, and any other place of public worship.

The Act prohibits the conversion of any place of worship from one religious denomination to another.

The Act also declares that the religious character of a place of worship shall continue to be the same as it existed on August 15, 1947.

Any suit or legal proceeding with respect to the conversion of the religious character of any place of worship existing on August 15, 1947, pending before any court, shall abate and no fresh suit or legal proceeding shall be instituted.

The Act has been used in various legal disputes, including the Kashi Vishwanath Temple-Gyanvapi mosque dispute, to determine the religious character of a place of worship.

The Act has been criticized for violating the principle of secularism enshrined in the Indian Constitution and for being an encroachment on the rights of religious communities.
